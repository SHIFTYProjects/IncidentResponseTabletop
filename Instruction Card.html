⚠️ BOTH GAMES ARE MEANT TO BE PROCTORED BY A LEADER TO FACILITATE DISCUSSION

Game 1: IT Focused Workshop

Budget: $500,000

1. Procurement Phase

Choose your software purchases carefully as a group. These are your only defenses against the incoming incidents.

  • You must select at least 1 from each category to begin.
  • Continue spending until your budget is exhausted or no further purchases can be made.

2. Engagement Phase

Click "Start Game" to advance and draw your first Inject Card. You will face a scenario where your defenses are tested:

+5 Points: Protected by your software -15 Points: Unprotected / No matching software

Proctor Discussion Points

  • Environment: Would you have been protected in your actual current environment?
  • Remediation: How do you fix this? How would you fix it in reality?
  • Scope: Which departments beyond IT are affected? Who needs to be notified?

After discussion, click "VIEW REAL WORLD IMPACT & REMEDIATION".

GOALS:

Create awareness of various environment outages and use the fillable sheets as a foundation to develop or realize robust backup plans. Can you survive 5 rounds?

Game 2: Emergency Response Game

Inter-Departmental

1. Configuration

  • Select Departments: Choose the groups participating or those you want to test.
  • Set Intensity: Choose the number of rounds and toggle the Timer for added urgency.
  • Click "Start Exercise" to reveal the first Inject.

2. Evaluation & Discussion

For each drawn Inject, use the fillable response worksheet to record findings as a team.

  • Response Rating: Use the slider to report the current plan status (from "No Plan" to "Fully Realized Plan").
  • Impact Mapping: Select all other departments affected by the specific incident.
  • Click "Submit & Next" to progress.

Post-Game Analysis

Once all rounds are complete, download the Final Summary Report to review ratings and survival metrics across all participating departments.

GOALS:

Highlight the interconnected nature of modern organizations. Understand that all departments are tied together—a failure in one often ripples through the entire environment.
